When the Fun Slipped Away

June 4, 20264-5 min readAnonymous Submission

Shared anonymously through the Broad Shoulders Initiative community submissions.

I played a sport for 10 years of my life, and I never felt good in any of those years. Throughout all my time playing, I watched people join years after me and improve greatly, even surpassing me. I never once was able to understand why it felt like I was always falling behind; everyone was better than me. That sport began to feel like a target that I was never able to hit. Everyone starts a sport because they want to have fun; however, while everyone else got better and was having fun doing so, the fun began to slip away for me, and I felt like my skill plateaued. Getting dropped off at practice felt like I was being taken to my execution. I had this pit in my stomach nonstop. It got to a point where I couldn't play at all. Every time I went onto the field, I would freeze up and would immediately be taken off. Finally, at 15 years old—ten years after I started playing—I decided it was time to stop. I began to play other sports and joined clubs in my free time. I branched out and made more friends, more connections, and overall, I enjoyed life more. Realizing it was time to quit was one of the best things that ever happened to me, and I am forever grateful that I was able to do so. Quitting is something that is very hard for anyone to do after they have invested a lot of time into it. The sunk cost fallacy is a very common phenomenon where individuals have an inherent bias to stick with something because they have invested so much time in it, rather than because it brings them value or happiness. The problem with this mindset is that if you truly don't like to do something, you should not force yourself to continue. Pursue the hobbies and tasks you genuinely enjoy, because, looking back decades later, it doesn't matter how long you did something for; it only matters if you stuck to it and continued loving it throughout your life. Do the things that make you happy, not the things that you believe should make you happy.
